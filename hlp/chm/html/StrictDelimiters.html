Strictdelimiters directive

Sometimes you may want a parser more fussy on data delimiters, consider for example the following data:
 a strange acquisition
  00  10  20
  01  11  21
  02  12  22
  03  13  23
      14  24
      15  25
      16  26
          27
          28
          29
With the default sloppy delimiters policy the file would be actually interpreted as:
 equivocation
  00  10  20
  01  11  21
  02  12  22
  03  13  23
  14  24
  15  25
  16  26
  27
  28
  29
This is a consequence of the ambiguous delimiter definition, which does not provide any knowledge on columns position. It is possible overcome this problem in two ways: the first trivial workaround is rearranging columns in this way:
 rearranged
  20   10   00
  21   11   01
  22   12   02
  23   13   03
  24   14
  25   15
  26   16
  27
  28          
  29
The second solution is declare a precise column delimiter, telling the parser to strictly expect a rigid data structure:
 forcing a rigid format
 #strictdelimiter ;
 00 ; 10 ; 20
 01 ; 11 ; 21
 02 ; 12 ; 22
 03 ; 13 ; 23
 ; 14 ; 24
 ; 15 ; 25
 ; 16 ; 26
 ; ; 27
 ; ; 28
 ; ; 29
Now parser is aware of empty columns, so column 0 will contain only four numbers. Note that spaces between delimiters and numbers are still allowed, while spaces between numbers will result in an invalid number error. As explicit developer choice the declared data delimiter must strictly be one char: a string delimiter would be a useless waste of space and parsing complexity.
